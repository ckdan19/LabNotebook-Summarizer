### Glycogen depletion under repeat thermal stress in Pacific oysters

**Source:** Genefish WordPress (Hazel Abrahamson, 2026-07-14)
**Finding:** A GlycogenGlo assay on oysters subjected to repeat temperature stress at 35°C found that both 24- and 48-hour heat exposures reduced glycogen levels, with 48-hour exposures causing substantial to complete mortality. Glycogen appeared capable of recovering after stress was removed, though high variability and small sample sizes prevented confirmation of a priming effect.

[PubMed] **Adds context**: Effects of temperature and *Nocardia crassostreae* on the immune response of the Pacific oyster, *Magallana gigas*
Mason et al., 2026 · PMID: 42476329 · https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42476329/

This study examined the combined effects of elevated temperature and bacterial infection on Pacific oysters, finding that elevated temperature independently reduced glycogen, ATP levels, and haemocyte viability — effects that occurred alongside, rather than by amplifying, bacterial immune challenges. The result shows that heat stress alone is sufficient to deplete glycogen and suppress physiological reserves in *M. gigas* (= *C. gigas*), consistent with the GlycogenGlo observations from the lab's repeat stress trials at 35°C. The specific energetic mechanism — heat stress consuming stored glycogen — mirrors what the lab is measuring, even though Mason et al. used a disease co-exposure design rather than a priming protocol.

**Literature summary:** One peer-reviewed paper published this week provides direct context for the GlycogenGlo results. Mason et al. (2026) independently document that elevated temperature reduces glycogen in *M. gigas*, consistent with the lab's observation that both 24- and 48-hour exposures at 35°C deplete glycogen reserves. The study does not address glycogen recovery after stress removal or repeated-stress priming, which remain open questions from the lab's experiment.
